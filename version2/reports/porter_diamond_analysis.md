# Porter's Diamond Interpretation
India State Competitiveness Index (ISCI) — Version 2.0

Michael Porter's Diamond is a simple idea about why some places are better for business
than others. It looks at four things:

- Factor Conditions: the basic inputs a place has, like educated people, health, power and
  roads.
- Demand Conditions: how big and how strong the local demand is.
- Related & Supporting Industries: the suppliers and connected industries around a firm.
- Firm Strategy, Structure & Rivalry: how firms are run and how hard they compete.

This report uses that idea to explain our index.

Two of the four are measured in the index: Factor Conditions and Related & Supporting
Industries. Demand Conditions is shown using income figures as a stand-in, which is not
part of the index. Firm Strategy is not measured, and we say so clearly.

Everything below comes from the index data. Where a sentence is our own explanation and
not just a plain number, we say so.

Sources: RBI Handbook of Statistics on Indian States 2024-25; Ministry of MSME Annual
Report.

## Factor Conditions

### What Porter means

Factor Conditions are the basic things a place gives its businesses: educated people, good
health, money to borrow, electricity and roads. In our index we measure them with seven
numbers: school enrolment, life expectancy, unemployment, the credit-to-deposit ratio,
power per person (how much electricity is available for each person), power lost on the
way (electricity lost between the power plant and homes, called transmission and
distribution losses), and road length per person (roads compared with the population,
called road density).

### Evidence from Version 1.0

The states that do best on Factor Conditions are Delhi, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh,
Maharashtra and Telangana. The ones that do worst are Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya,
Nagaland, Bihar and Uttar Pradesh.

Looking at all the ranked states, the numbers where states score highest (on the 0 to 1
scale) are low power loss (0.66) and low unemployment (0.63), then life expectancy (0.57).
The lowest are power per person (0.07) and road length per person (0.20).

When we compare each state's Factor Conditions score with its final score, they usually
rise and fall together. This closeness is 0.83 out of 1.

### Interpretation

Many of the top states are from the south and west of India, plus Delhi. The bottom group
has Bihar, Uttar Pradesh and three north-eastern states.

Power per person and road length per person have much lower average scores than the other
numbers. Their shapes are studied in Notebook 06 and may change how states are spread out
on these two.

States with higher Factor Conditions scores also tend to have higher final scores (0.83).
But two numbers moving together does not prove that one causes the other.

### Strengths

- Low power loss has the highest average score among the seven Factor Conditions numbers.
- Unemployment and life expectancy also have fairly high average scores.

### Weaknesses

- Power per person and road length per person have the lowest average scores among the
  seven.
- Bihar and Uttar Pradesh, the two biggest northern states, are near the bottom here.

### Open questions

- Why do Bihar and Uttar Pradesh score low here even though they are so big?
- Why is power per person so high in only a few states?
- How much of a state's final rank comes from Factor Conditions, and how much from
  industry?
- Which of these seven numbers create the biggest gap between the top and bottom states?

## Related & Supporting Industries

### What Porter means

These are the suppliers and connected industries that help a firm do well. In our index we
measure them with four numbers: factory density, MSME density, the manufacturing share of
output, and the investment rate.

### Evidence from Version 1.0

The states that do best are Gujarat, Goa, Tamil Nadu, Puducherry and Haryana. The ones
that do worst are Nagaland, Arunachal Pradesh, Andaman & Nicobar Islands, Mizoram and
Manipur.

Across the ranked states, the highest average score is on MSME density (0.45), then the
manufacturing share (0.37), factory density (0.36) and the investment rate (0.31).

When we compare each state's score here with its final score, they rise and fall together
very closely. This closeness is 0.93 out of 1. The scores on this part are also much more
spread out than on Factor Conditions.

### Interpretation

The top states are industrial states from the west and south, led by Gujarat and Goa. The
bottom group is made up of small north-eastern states.

This part is more closely tied to the final score (0.93) than Factor Conditions (0.83),
and its scores are more spread out. States that do well here also tend to rank higher
overall. Again, moving together does not prove cause.

Because the scores here are more spread out, the differences between states on industry
matter a lot for the final order, even though every number carries the same weight.

### Strengths

- MSME density has the highest average score of the four numbers.
- The top states, Gujarat and Goa, score far above the average on the manufacturing share
  and factory density.

### Weaknesses

- The investment rate has the lowest average score of the four.
- The small north-eastern states score near the bottom on this part.

### Open questions

- Why is industry packed into only a few western and southern states?
- Can a state climb the ranking mostly by building up its industry?
- Which of the four numbers separates the top and bottom states the most?

## Demand Conditions

### What Porter means

Demand Conditions are about how big and how strong the local demand is. A big or demanding
home market can push firms to get better. This part is not in the index. We describe it
here with two official income figures as stand-ins: total NSDP for the size of a state's
economy, and per-capita NSDP for how much people earn on average. These are only for
context and do not change the index.

### Evidence from Version 1.0 data (stand-in)

By size of economy (total NSDP, 2022-23), the biggest are Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu,
Karnataka, Uttar Pradesh and Gujarat. The smallest are the little union territories and
north-eastern states.

By income per person (per-capita NSDP, 2022-23), the highest are Goa, Sikkim, Delhi,
Chandigarh and Telangana. The lowest are Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand, Manipur and
Assam.

Both figures move with the competitiveness score, but not very strongly: 0.53 for income
per person and 0.42 for size of economy.

### Interpretation

The two stand-ins do not always agree. Uttar Pradesh has one of the biggest economies but
one of the lowest incomes per person. Small states like Goa and Sikkim have small
economies but high income per person.

States with higher income per person tend to have higher competitiveness scores (0.53),
and bigger economies also tend to score higher (0.42). Both links are weaker than the two
parts that are in the index. And moving together does not prove cause.

### Strengths (as a stand-in)

- The income figures cover all 33 ranked states for 2022-23, so demand can be described in
  the same way for every state.

### Weaknesses (as a stand-in)

- Income and size are only rough stand-ins. Porter's Demand Conditions also mean how
  demanding and choosy local buyers are, and there is no official state-level data on that
  in Version 2.0.

### Open questions

- Does a bigger home market help a state build stronger industry?
- Why do some high-income states, like Goa and Sikkim, not have big industry?

## Firm Strategy, Structure & Rivalry

### What Porter means

This part is about how firms are run and how hard they compete with each other. Strong
local competition can push firms to improve.

### Status in Version 2.0

This part is only partly measured, because there is no suitable official state-level data
for it in Version 2.0. The index has no direct measure of how firms compete.

One official source could give a stand-in later: the DPIIT Startup India state-wise data
(Annual Report 2025-26) counts recognised startups by state, which could stand for
business energy and competition. We do not use it here because pulling a clean state-level
table out of that report is a separate job with its own checks, and rushing it would lower
the quality of the work.

### Interpretation

We make no claim about how firms compete across states in Version 2.0. The two measured
parts already show some of the industry picture, but they do not measure competition
between firms.

### Open questions

- Can startup counts be a fair stand-in for firm competition across states?
- What other official data could measure how firms compete at the state level?

## Overall Porter Diamond Assessment

### What the Diamond looks like in Version 1.0

Out of Porter's four parts, two are measured in the index (Factor Conditions and Related &
Supporting Industries), one is shown with stand-ins (Demand Conditions), and one is not
measured (Firm Strategy). So the Diamond is only partly filled in.

Of the two measured parts, Related & Supporting Industries drives the ranking more. It is
more closely tied to the final score (0.93 against 0.83) and its scores are more spread
out.

The two measured parts are somewhat linked to each other (0.58). Five states are strong on
both, sitting in the top 10 of each: Goa, Puducherry, Punjab, Tamil Nadu and Telangana.
Seven states are weak on both, sitting in the bottom 10 of each: Arunachal Pradesh, Bihar,
Jammu & Kashmir, Manipur, Meghalaya, Nagaland and Uttar Pradesh. Some states lean one way:
Delhi is strong on Factor Conditions but not on industry, while Gujarat is strong on
industry but not among the top on Factor Conditions.

Demand Conditions, shown only through income stand-ins, is tied to competitiveness more
weakly (0.53 and 0.42) than the two parts in the index.

### What this means for reading the ranking

A state's place is shaped mostly by its industry, helped by its basic conditions. States
strong on both parts tend to sit high, and states weak on both tend to sit low.

### Limits of this assessment

- Only two of the four parts are in the index.
- Demand is shown with stand-ins, not measured in the index.
- Firm Strategy is not measured.

So this covers two of Porter's four parts with real data, and part of a third with
stand-ins. We say this openly so it is not read as a full Diamond study.
