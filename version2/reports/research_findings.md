# Research Findings
India State Competitiveness Index (ISCI) — Version 2.0

**Research question:** What are the main findings across the whole project?

This report adds no new analysis. It gathers the findings that Notebooks 06 to 13 already
established, and puts them in one place. Each finding names the notebook it comes from, so it
can be checked.

## Finding 1: States differ in overall competitiveness

The index scores 33 states and union territories. Scores run from about 0.20 to 0.61. Goa is
the highest and Arunachal Pradesh the lowest. The scores are close together, so the order of
the states matters more than the gaps between the scores. (Based on Notebooks 04 and 07.)

## Finding 2: States fall into three groups

Using only the two measured parts, the states form three clear groups: strong on both parts
(12 states), strong basic conditions but weaker industry (6 states), and weak on both parts
(15 states). The only real difference between the top group and the middle group is industry.
(Based on Notebook 10.)

## Finding 3: Basic conditions shape today's position

Compared with the national average, most states' biggest weakness is a basic-conditions
number. This is true for 23 of the 33 states, against 10 on industry. So where a state stands
today, against the average, is shaped mostly by basic conditions like health, schooling,
jobs and roads. (Based on Notebook 11.)

## Finding 4: Industry sets the distance to the top

Compared with the top group, the picture flips. The biggest gap is on industry for 26 of the
33 states. In the same way, an industry-related area is the main priority for 26 of the 33
states. So the distance from an average state to the strongest states is mostly about
industry. (Based on Notebooks 11 and 12.)

## Finding 5: Closing one gap helps but does not transform a state

When a state closes its single biggest gap, its score rises by a small amount, between 0.005
and 0.08, and its rank moves by 0 to 6 places. Some middle-ranked states move the most,
because many states have similar scores. The weakest states improve but stay near the bottom,
because they still have several remaining gaps. (Based on Notebook 13.)

## The overall picture

Putting these together:

- Basic conditions explain where a state stands against the average today.
- Industry explains the remaining distance to the strongest states.
- The three groups differ mainly on industry, not on basic conditions.
- Improving one area helps a little, but most states would need gains in more than one area
  to move much further up.

None of this is a new claim. Each point is a summary of what the earlier notebooks measured.

## Limits

- The index measures two of Porter's four parts (Factor Conditions and Related & Supporting
  Industries). Demand is only described with income figures, and firm competition is not
  measured.
- The index uses only official data from the RBI Handbook and the MSME report. It does not
  include every factor that affects development.
- Some indicators are missing for smaller states, and small groups can move up or down more
  easily.
- The scenarios are simple simulations, not forecasts.
- The findings show links in the data. They do not prove cause.
