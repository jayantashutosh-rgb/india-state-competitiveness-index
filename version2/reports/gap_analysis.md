# Gap Analysis
India State Competitiveness Index (ISCI) — Version 2.0

**Research question:** What are the biggest gaps, and how far is each state from the top?

This report measures the gaps. It does not explain why the gaps exist or how they should be
reduced. Those questions are covered in the next notebooks.

## Two benchmarks

We measure each state's gaps against two benchmarks:

- The national average. This shows the state's current position: where it is below or above
  the average of all states.
- The top group (the "Strong on both parts" states from Notebook 10). This is an
  improvement target: how far a state is from the strongest states.

A positive gap means the state is below the benchmark. All numbers are on the 0 to 1 score
scale. The detailed gap tables are available in gap_vs_national.csv and gap_vs_top_group.csv,
and a short per-state table is in state_gap_diagnostic.csv.

## Gap vs the national average (current position)

For each state we find the number where it is furthest below the national average. This is
its current weakness.

The numbers that are most often a state's biggest weakness are:

- Manufacturing share and life expectancy (6 states each)
- Unemployment and road density (5 states each)
- School enrolment (4 states)

So against the average, weaknesses are spread across many different numbers.

## Gap vs the top group (improvement target)

For each state we find the number where it is furthest below the top group. This is its
development gap.

Here the pattern is much clearer. For 24 of the 33 states, the biggest development gap is on
factory density, investment or manufacturing:

- Factory density (11 states)
- Investment rate (7 states)
- Manufacturing share (6 states)

So 24 of the 33 states have their biggest gap to the top group on factory density,
investment or manufacturing.

## Smallest gaps

For each state we also find its easiest gap: the smallest gap it needs to close to reach the
top group.

The most common easiest gaps are per-capita power (13 states), road density (6) and the
credit-to-deposit ratio (4).

A note of care: per-capita power comes up most often because the top group itself scores low
on it (0.12). The gap is small because the target is low, not because power is easy to
improve. A small gap does not always mean an easy improvement. It only means the state is
already closer to the benchmark on that number.

## Patterns across states

- Against the national average, states' biggest weaknesses are mostly in basic conditions:
  23 of 33 states, against 10 on industry.
- Against the top group, the gap flips to industry: 26 of 33 states have their biggest
  development gap on factory density, investment, manufacturing or MSMEs.
- Most states are already at or above the top group on unemployment, road density, life
  expectancy and schooling. Fewest reach the top group's level on the industry numbers.
- So the weak spot depends on the benchmark. Against the average, it is often basic
  conditions. Against the strongest states, it is almost always industry.

The two benchmarks answer different questions, so they do not always point to the same
weakness.
