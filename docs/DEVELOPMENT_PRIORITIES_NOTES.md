# Development Priorities Notes (Notebook 12)
India State Competitiveness Index (ISCI)

---

## What this notebook is for

This notebook shows where each state should focus to improve. It uses the gaps from
Notebook 11 to point out the areas where a state is furthest behind the strongest states.

It is careful about one thing. It shows priority areas, not policies. The data can show
where a state is behind. It cannot show which scheme a government should run.

## Why this notebook was needed

We measured the gaps in Notebook 11. But a long list of gaps is hard to act on. This
notebook picks out, for each state and each type of state, the areas that matter most. It
turns many numbers into a short, clear set of priorities.

## What we learned

For most states, the main priority is industry. Out of 33 states, 26 have an
industry-related area (industry, manufacturing, investment or MSMEs) as their biggest gap
to the top group. States with strong basic conditions but weak industry, like Kerala and
Delhi, point clearly to investment and manufacturing.

## The most important rule

This notebook gives priorities, not prescriptions. It says which areas are furthest behind.
It does not say what action to take. That is a separate question, and it is not in this
data.

## From numbers to areas

Each indicator is linked to a broad area, so the priorities are easy to read:

- Industry = factory density
- Manufacturing = manufacturing share
- Investment = investment rate
- MSMEs = MSME density
- Jobs = low unemployment
- Health = life expectancy
- Education = school enrolment
- Finance = credit-to-deposit ratio
- Power supply = per-capita power
- Power losses = transmission and distribution losses
- Roads = road density

## What each cell does and what it found

- Setup:
  Opens the ranking, the state groups, and the gaps to the top group. Sets the map from
  each number to a broad area.

- Priorities by state type:
  For each of the three groups, counts the numbers that appear most often among its states'
  two biggest gaps.
  Found: the strong group has small mixed gaps. The middle group points clearly to
  investment and manufacturing. The weak group points to industry first, with education for
  a few states.

- Priorities by state:
  For each state, lists its main priority, second priority and smallest gap, as broad
  areas. Saves the table.
  Found: most states have an industry-related main priority.

- Cross-state findings:
  Counts how often each area is a state's main priority.
  Found: industry (11), investment (7) and manufacturing (6) lead. Together with MSMEs, 26
  of the 33 states have an industry-related main priority.

The written report, version2/reports/development_priorities.md, is built from these numbers.

## What we understood overall

- For most states, the biggest gap to the top group is in industry.
- States that already have strong basic conditions but weak industry mostly need investment
  and manufacturing.
- A "smallest gap" area is only where a state is already close to the top group. It is not
  always the easiest thing to improve.

## FAQ

### General

What is a priority area?
The area where a state is furthest behind the top group. It is where improvement would
close the most of the gap.

Why not give actual policies?
Because the data cannot prove which policy works. It can only show where a state is behind.
Giving a scheme would go beyond what the data can support.

Does this notebook change the index?
No. It reads the ranking, the groups and the gaps, and turns them into priorities.

### The priorities

Why is industry the priority for so many states?
Because the biggest gaps to the top group are on the industry numbers, as Notebook 11
showed. States that want to reach the top are furthest behind on industry.

Why do the strong states have small, mixed priorities?
They are already near the top, so their remaining gaps are small and spread across
different areas.

Why split power into "supply" and "losses"?
Two different numbers were both called "power": per-capita power and power losses. Keeping
them together made a state's biggest and smallest gap look like the same area. Splitting
them keeps each state's priorities clear.

### Limits

Do these priorities tell a government what to do?
No. They point to areas, not actions. They also use only the numbers in this index, not
every factor that affects development. A state may have other important priorities that are
not measured by this index.
